1Nature of Futures Trading

Futures contracts are legally binding agreements to buy or sell a specific commodity, financial instrument, or index at a predetermined price on a specified future date. Futures trading is conducted on regulated exchanges and involves significant risk that may not be appropriate for all investors.

Trading in futures contracts — including but not limited to equity index futures (ES, NQ, YM, RTY), energy futures (CL, NG), metals futures (GC, SI), and interest rate futures (ZB, ZN) — involves the risk of losing all or more than the initial investment. These markets can move rapidly and the potential for loss may be unlimited in certain situations.

Important: Unlike stock investments, futures contracts require you to maintain a margin deposit that is only a fraction of the total contract value. This means even a small adverse price movement can result in losses that significantly exceed your initial deposit.

2Leverage & Margin Risk

Futures trading is highly leveraged. The margin required to establish a futures position is typically between 3% and 12% of the total notional value of the contract. This high degree of leverage means that a relatively small market movement will have a proportionately larger impact on the funds deposited as margin.

  • Margin calls: If the market moves against your position, you may be required to deposit substantial additional funds on short notice to maintain the position. Failure to do so may result in the forced liquidation of your position at a loss.
  • Excess losses: In fast-moving markets, it is possible to lose more than the total amount deposited with your broker. You will be liable for any deficit in your account.
  • Overnight risk: Positions held overnight are exposed to gap risk — the risk that prices open significantly higher or lower than the previous close due to news or events occurring outside of trading hours.
  • Micro and mini contracts: While micro (MES, MNQ) and mini contracts reduce the nominal contract size, the proportional risks remain the same. Leverage still applies.

3Market Volatility Risk

Futures markets can experience extreme and sudden price movements due to a wide range of factors including, but not limited to:

Economic Events
Federal Reserve decisions, employment reports, GDP data, CPI releases, and other macroeconomic announcements can cause sharp, immediate price movements.
🌍
Geopolitical Events
Wars, political instability, elections, trade disputes, and sanctions can create sudden and prolonged volatility across all futures markets.
🔒
Circuit Breakers
Exchanges may impose trading halts, limit up/down price restrictions, or circuit breakers that can prevent you from exiting a position at a desired price.
⚠
Flash Crashes
Rapid, deep market sell-offs can occur within seconds, triggering stop-loss orders at prices far below their intended levels (slippage).
Liquidity Events
During periods of extreme market stress, bid/ask spreads may widen significantly, making it costly or impossible to execute trades at expected prices.
⏰
After-Hours Risk
Futures trade nearly 24 hours a day, five days a week. Adverse movements during low-liquidity overnight sessions may significantly impact open positions.

4Algorithmic & Automated Trading Risk

AutoFuturesTrader provides algorithmic trading tools and strategies that execute trades automatically on your behalf. While automation removes emotional decision-making, it introduces a unique set of risks that all users must understand:

  • Algorithm failure: Trading algorithms may contain programming errors, logic flaws, or may behave unexpectedly in unusual market conditions not anticipated during development or backtesting.
  • Backtesting limitations: Historical performance results achieved through backtesting have inherent limitations. Past simulated results do not guarantee future live trading results. Backtests are conducted with the benefit of hindsight and cannot fully account for real-world execution factors including slippage, commissions, and liquidity constraints.
  • Over-optimization: Strategies that have been optimized on historical data ("curve-fitted") may perform poorly in live markets due to changing market conditions.
  • Model risk: The quantitative models underlying our trading strategies involve assumptions about market behavior. If those assumptions prove incorrect, the strategy may generate losses.
  • Execution risk: Actual fills may differ from theoretical fills due to latency, broker execution policies, and market impact.
  • Autonomous operation: Once deployed, automated systems will continue to place trades without manual intervention. Users are responsible for monitoring their accounts and halting execution if market conditions warrant.
User Responsibility: You remain solely responsible for your trading account at all times. AutoFuturesTrader provides technology tools, not investment advice. You must actively monitor all automated activity and maintain sufficient capital in your account to meet margin requirements.

5Liquidity Risk

Not all futures markets maintain deep liquidity at all times. Liquidity risk refers to the risk that a trader may not be able to execute a trade at the desired price or time due to insufficient market activity.

Specific liquidity risks include:

  • Wide bid/ask spreads during off-hours or low-volume periods
  • Inability to exit a position quickly at a reasonable price during fast markets or market dislocations
  • Reduced liquidity near contract expiration dates, particularly for deferred-month contracts
  • Position size relative to average daily volume — large positions may move the market against you upon entry or exit

6Technology & System Risk

The use of internet-based and electronic trading systems carries inherent technology risks that may result in losses:

  • Platform outages: AutoFuturesTrader's platform, your broker's platform, or the exchange itself may experience downtime, preventing order entry, modification, or cancellation.
  • Internet connectivity: Loss of internet connection may prevent the algorithm from placing, modifying, or closing orders, leaving positions unmanaged.
  • Data feed errors: Incorrect or delayed market data may cause the algorithm to make erroneous trading decisions.
  • Cybersecurity: Unauthorized access to trading accounts, although protected by multiple security layers, may result in unauthorized trades or account compromise.
  • Third-party dependencies: The system relies on broker APIs, exchange connectivity, and data providers. Failure in any of these third-party services may disrupt trading operations.

AutoFuturesTrader maintains redundant infrastructure and monitoring systems; however, we cannot guarantee uninterrupted service and shall not be liable for losses resulting from technology failures outside our reasonable control.

7Regulatory & Legal Risk

Futures trading is subject to extensive federal regulation. Changes in applicable laws, regulations, rules, or exchange policies may adversely affect the ability to execute certain strategies or maintain certain positions. Regulatory risks include:

  • Changes in position limits imposed by the CFTC or exchanges
  • New margin requirements imposed by exchanges or the Federal Reserve (Regulation T)
  • Changes in tax treatment of futures trading gains and losses under Section 1256 of the Internal Revenue Code
  • Exchange rule changes affecting order types, fees, or execution priority
  • Broker insolvency or regulatory action against your clearing firm

8CFTC Regulatory Framework

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) is an independent federal agency that regulates the U.S. derivatives markets, including futures, swaps, and certain kinds of options. The CFTC's mission is to promote the integrity, resilience, and vibrancy of the U.S. derivatives markets through sound regulation.

CFTC Commodity Exchange Act (CEA)

All futures trading in the United States is governed by the Commodity Exchange Act (CEA), 7 U.S.C. §§ 1 et seq. The CEA establishes the regulatory framework for futures exchanges, commodity pool operators, commodity trading advisors, introducing brokers, and futures commission merchants.

CFTC Rule 4.41 — Hypothetical Performance Results

Pursuant to CFTC Rule 4.41, hypothetical or simulated performance results have certain inherent limitations. Unlike an actual performance record, simulated results do not represent actual trading. Also, since the trades have not been executed, the results may have under- or over-compensated for the impact, if any, of certain market factors, such as lack of liquidity. Simulated trading programs in general are also subject to the fact that they are designed with the benefit of hindsight. No representation is being made that any account will or is likely to achieve profits or losses similar to those shown.

CFTC Rule 1.55 — Risk Disclosure Statement

Before opening an account to trade futures or options on futures, a futures commission merchant (FCM) or introducing broker (IB) must provide you with this risk disclosure statement. You should read this entire statement carefully before opening an account. Keep this document in a safe place for future reference.

CFTC Whistleblower Program

The CFTC Whistleblower Program rewards individuals who provide original information about violations of the Commodity Exchange Act. If you believe you have been the victim of fraud or have information about fraud, manipulation, or other wrongdoing, you may report it to the CFTC at www.cftc.gov/whistleblower or by calling 1-866-366-2382.

9National Futures Association (NFA) Compliance

The National Futures Association (NFA) is the self-regulatory organization for the U.S. derivatives industry. The NFA develops rules, programs, and services to safeguard market integrity, protect investors, and help its members meet their regulatory responsibilities.

NFA BASIC — Background Affiliation Status Information Center

You can verify the registration status and disciplinary history of any CFTC-registered firm or individual by using the NFA's Background Affiliation Status Information Center (BASIC) at www.nfa.futures.org/BasicNet.

NFA Investor Resources

The NFA provides educational resources for futures investors, including information on fraud prevention, understanding your account statement, and knowing your rights as a customer. These resources are available at www.nfa.futures.org.

Futures Investor Protection

Customer funds held at CFTC-registered FCMs are held in segregated accounts separate from the firm's own funds. However, unlike the FDIC or SIPC protections available for bank and securities accounts, there is no government insurance fund that covers futures customer accounts in the event of an FCM bankruptcy. Investors should carefully evaluate the financial condition of any FCM with whom they open an account.
